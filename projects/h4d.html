Ad Astra: DoD-NASA Logistics Interoperability

  • Program: Berkeley Security and Resilience Accelerator / Hacking for Defense
  • Sponsor: SpaceWERX
  • Relevant Skills: Hacking for Defense, Lean LaunchPad, Mission Model Canvas, Stakeholder Discovery, Defense Logistics, Space Logistics, Systems Analysis, Policy Analysis, Risk & Mission Impact Assessment, Government Briefing & Presentation

Background

Ad Astra was completed as part of an intensive semester-long Berkeley Security and Resilience Accelerator course based on the Hacking for Defense curriculum. The program applied Lean LaunchPad methods to real national-security problems, requiring weekly stakeholder discovery, hypothesis testing, Mission Model Canvas iteration, and briefings to mentors and problem sponsors.

The class project is complete, and the team is continuing the work outside the course. During the semester, the project focused on a SpaceWERX-sponsored challenge at the intersection of defense logistics, civil space operations, and future joint or shared missions.

Problem

Defense and space organizations use different logistics languages. The Department of Defense traditionally organizes sustainment around a ten-class supply taxonomy, while NASA uses six functional logistics categories shaped by spaceflight operations and mission life-cycle needs. These systems were built for different operating environments, governance structures, and risk profiles.

For future missions involving shared infrastructure, cislunar operations, or deeper DoD-NASA coordination, mismatched logistics categories can make it harder to compare needs, assign responsibility, prioritize critical items, and plan sustainment across organizations.

Methodology

  • Lean LaunchPad: Treated assumptions about users, beneficiaries, buyers, and mission value as testable hypotheses rather than fixed requirements.
  • Mission Model Canvas: Mapped stakeholders, value propositions, mission achievement, adoption barriers, deployment pathways, and support networks.
  • Stakeholder Discovery: Conducted recurring conversations with defense, intelligence, space-sector, and logistics stakeholders to refine the problem framing.
  • Iterative Briefings: Synthesized findings into weekly updates and final course deliverables for instructors, mentors, and the sponsor community.

Research Focus

The project compared the Department of Defense and NASA logistics systems across function, criticality, governance, and life-cycle intent. Instead of treating the categories as simple one-to-one equivalents, the work examined where each system encodes different assumptions about mission risk, operational ownership, and sustainment over time.

  • Mapped defense supply classes against NASA functional logistics categories
  • Identified alignment, ambiguity, and mismatch between taxonomy layers
  • Analyzed where terminology gaps could affect shared mission planning
  • Developed recommendations toward a common framework for interoperability

Work Performed

  • Built and refined a Mission Model Canvas around the logistics interoperability problem
  • Conducted stakeholder interviews to test whether the taxonomy mismatch represented a real operational pain point
  • Compared supply-chain language across military logistics, NASA operations, and space-sector sustainment needs
  • Prepared executive and presentation materials summarizing the problem, evidence, and proposed direction
  • Briefed findings through the accelerator format and incorporated feedback into the final recommendation set

Outcome

The semester deliverables framed logistics taxonomy mismatch as a practical interoperability issue for future joint space missions. The final recommendations pointed toward a shared framework that can preserve the intent of each organization’s logistics model while making criticality, ownership, and life-cycle assumptions easier to compare.

The project also produced a stronger understanding of how national-security innovation work differs from conventional product development: the core challenge was not only building a solution, but validating whose mission would be improved, what adoption path would be realistic, and where an intervention could fit inside a government ecosystem.

The class project was completed during the semester. The team is continuing Ad Astra outside the course to further develop the work beyond the accelerator.
