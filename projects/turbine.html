Miniature Wind Turbine

Summary: A semester-long project to design, 3D print, and test a small-scale wind turbine. Focused on aerodynamics, structural design, and renewable energy performance. Conducted under ENGIN 26: Three-Dimensional Modeling for Design at UC Berkeley.

Requirements

  • Power output > 2 W at 25 mph wind
  • Stiffness > 8 N/mm
  • Total weight < 350 g
  • 16-inch tower height, max blade length 3 inches
  • Modular design for 3D printing (ABS plastic)

Results

  • Peak Power: 0.3908 W
  • Stiffness: >8 N/mm (0.94 mm deflection under 1 kg load)
  • Mass: 253.3 g
  • Efficiency: 3.19%

Blade Design

  • 3 blades, each 2.5” in length
  • Twist angle: 21°, Angle of attack: 15°
  • Symmetric airfoil profile for stability
  • Created in SolidWorks using loft & pattern tools

Tower Design

  • Eiffel-inspired 3-legged structure for stability
  • Equilateral triangular cross-section, tapered top
  • Modular split for 3D printing, male-female couplings
  • FEA showed max displacement 0.2145 mm, FOS > 3

Skills

CAD & FEA (SolidWorks), Aerodynamics, Rapid Prototyping, Physical Testing, Technical Documentation, Team Collaboration

Reflection & Improvements

The Miniature Wind Turbine project successfully demonstrated the design, fabrication, and testing of a small-scale renewable energy system. The prototype met key structural requirements such as stiffness and weight while showcasing functional aerodynamic performance. However, efficiency and power output remained below the target threshold, highlighting areas for improvement.

  • Blade Aerodynamics: Refining the blade profile and testing alternative airfoil geometries could significantly increase power output.
  • Material Selection: While ABS plastic was suitable for prototyping, switching to composites or lightweight metals could improve durability and stiffness.
  • Generator Integration: Optimizing the coupling between the blades and generator would reduce mechanical losses and improve efficiency.
  • Tower Stability: The Eiffel-inspired structure performed well, but incorporating damping elements could further reduce vibration during high wind speeds.
  • Testing Conditions: Expanding tests beyond laboratory setups (e.g., outdoor wind conditions) would provide more realistic performance data.

Overall, the project provided valuable experience in CAD modeling, finite element analysis, and hands-on prototyping. Future iterations could focus on improving aerodynamic performance and energy conversion efficiency while maintaining structural reliability.
